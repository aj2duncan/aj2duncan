--- title: "The Onion Method for Rearranging Equations" date: "2017-11-08" slug: "the-onion-method" draft: true ---

The other day in the office we were discussing methods of explaining how to change the subject of a formula, our different approaches and any alternatives we have. One difficulty that students can have is the order in which to rearrange and one of my colleagues demonstrated the onion method which might help.

If we take an example of the equation below, where we want to change the subject to \(t\).

\[ \require{enclose} v = ut + a \] The onion method starts by drawing a circle around the new subject. \[v = u\enclose{circle}{\:t\:} + a.\]

Then we circle the term closest to it

\[v = \enclose{circle}{\:u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}\:} + a\]

and continue with this until all the terms are circled

\[v = \enclose{circle}{\:\enclose{circle}{\:u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}\:} + a\:}.\]

To rearrange we peel away the layers, so we start with the largest circle.

\[ \begin{align} v &= \enclose{circle}{\:\enclose{circle}{\:u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}\:} + a\:} \\ v - a &= \enclose{circle}{\:\enclose{circle}{\:u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}\:} + a - a\:}\\ v - a &= \enclose{circle}{\:u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}\:} \end{align} \]

We continue with this approach

\[ \begin{align} v - a &= \enclose{circle}{\:u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}\:} \\ \frac{v - a}{u} &= \enclose{circle}{\:\frac{u\:\enclose{circle}{\:t\:}}{u}\:} \\ \frac{v - a}{u} &= \enclose{circle}{\:t\:}} \end{align} \]

In this case we are now finished but it works for more complicated examples too.
